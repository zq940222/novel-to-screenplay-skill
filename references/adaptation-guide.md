## Core Adaptation Principles

### Show, Don't Tell
Novels tell; screenplays show. Convert every piece of internal narration into:
- Observable action (facial expressions, body language, physical choices)
- Dialogue that reveals character indirectly
- Visual metaphor or symbolic action
- Environmental storytelling

**Novel:**
> Sarah felt a wave of guilt wash over her. She had never meant to hurt anyone, but here she was, standing in the wreckage of her own making.

**Screenplay:**
```
Sarah stares at the shattered vase on the floor. Her hand trembles. She kneels, carefully picks up a shard, then sets it down — unable to even begin fixing what she's broken.
```

### Compression is Key
- A 30,000-word novel has ~100,000 words of "content" (description + dialogue + narration)
- A 90-page screenplay has ~15,000-18,000 words
- Compression ratio: roughly 6:1
- Not everything survives — prioritize what serves the VISUAL story

### Fidelity vs. Cinematic Quality
Faithful adaptation ≠ literal translation. Preserve:
- **Emotional truth** of the story
- **Character essence** (motivations, arcs, relationships)
- **Thematic core**
- **Key dramatic moments**

Freely change:
- Number of characters (combine or cut)
- Subplot count (reduce to 2-3 max)
- Chronological order (if it serves drama)
- Setting details (if visual equivalent works better)
- Exposition method (narration → visual/dialogue)

## Analysis Phase

Before writing, extract these elements from the novel:

### 1. Story Spine
- **Protagonist**: Who drives the story? What do they want?
- **Antagonist/Opposition**: What prevents them?
- **Central conflict**: Internal? External? Both?
- **Stakes**: What happens if protagonist fails?
- **Theme**: What is the story really about?

### 2. Plot Architecture
Map the novel's events to three-act structure:
- **Inciting incident**: What disrupts the status quo?
- **First turning point**: Point of no return
- **Midpoint**: Major revelation or reversal
- **Second turning point**: All seems lost / darkest moment
- **Climax**: Final confrontation
- **Resolution**: New equilibrium

### 3. Character Inventory
For each character, determine:
- Essential to the plot? → Keep
- Provides unique function no other character serves? → Keep
- Can be merged with another character? → Merge
- Exists only for subplot that won't make the cut? → Cut

Target: 5-8 significant characters for a 90-minute film.

### 4. Scene Inventory
List every scene/chapter with:
- Location
- Characters present
- Key event/information conveyed
- Emotional beat
- Essential (Y/N) for main plot

## Structure Mapping

### Novel Chapter → Screenplay Sequence

| Novel Element | Screenplay Equivalent |
|--------------|----------------------|
| Chapter | Sequence (3-5 scenes) |
| Paragraph of description | 1-2 action lines |
| Internal monologue | Visual behavior / subtext dialogue |
| Backstory chapter | Brief flashback or dialogue hint |
| Letter/document | CLOSE ON document or V.O. reading |
| Dream sequence | Stylized visual sequence |
| Multiple POVs | Intercut sequences |

### Pacing Guidelines (90-minute film)

| Section | Pages | Scenes | Purpose |
|---------|-------|--------|---------|
| Opening | 1-5 | 1-3 | Hook, establish world/tone |
| Setup | 6-15 | 4-8 | Character, world, status quo |
| Inciting incident | 15-18 | 1-2 | Disruption |
| Act 1 turning point | 20-25 | 1-2 | Protagonist commits |
| Fun & games / Rising | 25-45 | 8-12 | Promise of the premise |
| Midpoint | 45-50 | 1-2 | Stakes raise / false victory or defeat |
| Bad guys close in | 50-60 | 5-8 | Complications escalate |
| All is lost | 60-65 | 1-2 | Darkest moment |
| Act 2 turning point | 65-70 | 1-2 | New insight / final push |
| Finale | 70-85 | 5-8 | Climax sequence |
| Resolution | 85-90 | 1-3 | New normal |

Total: ~40-55 scenes for a 90-minute film.

## Scene Conversion Techniques

### Technique 1: Distillation
Take a 2,000-word chapter and ask: what is the ONE thing this scene must accomplish? Write only that.

### Technique 2: Visual Substitution
Replace abstract concepts with concrete visual symbols.
- "Time passed" → MONTAGE
- "She missed him" → She reaches for his coffee mug, stops, puts it back
- "The town was dying" → Boarded-up storefronts, empty playground

### Technique 3: Scene Fusion
Combine 2-3 novel scenes with similar purposes into one cinematic scene.
- Novel: 3 separate conversations revealing a secret → Screenplay: 1 dinner scene where it all unravels

### Technique 4: Externalization
Convert internal conflict to external, observable conflict.
- Novel: Character debates internally for pages → Screenplay: Character argues with someone who represents the other side of the debate

## Dialogue Adaptation

### Novel Dialogue → Screen Dialogue
- Cut exposition dumps — characters don't explain what they both already know
- Shorten: novel dialogue is often 2x too long for screen
- Add subtext: what characters mean ≠ what they say
- Make each character's voice distinct (vocabulary, rhythm, verbal tics)
- Use silence and action beats between lines

**Novel dialogue (expository):**
> "As you know, Sarah, we've been partners for ten years, ever since we met at the academy. And now, with the new chief taking over and threatening to shut down our unit..."

**Screen dialogue (with subtext):**
```
                         JACK
            You talk to the new chief yet?

                         SARAH
            He doesn't want to talk.

                         JACK
            Ten years, Sarah.

Sarah looks away.

                         SARAH
            I know how long it's been.
```

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Voice-over addiction**: V.O. narration is a crutch. Use only when it adds style (noir, unreliable narrator) not information.
2. **Talking heads**: Two people in a room talking for 3 pages. Add physical action, change locations, create visual interest.
3. **Faithful but boring**: Including scenes because they're in the novel, not because they're cinematic.
4. **Character overload**: Keeping all 15 novel characters. Merge ruthlessly.
5. **Flashback overuse**: More than 2-3 flashbacks in 90 minutes feels fragmented.
6. **On-the-nose dialogue**: Characters stating their feelings directly instead of showing them through behavior and subtext.
